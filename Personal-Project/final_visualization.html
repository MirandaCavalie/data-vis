1997 – 2000

The Crisis Begins

San Francisco's first tech boom unleashed a wave of displacement. Venture-backed startups filled offices while landlords discovered they could earn far more replacing long-term tenants.


1998: 3,865 evictions — a peak unseen for 15 years. Owner move-in and breach notices dominated. The Mission and SoMa darkened on the map.

2001 – 2007

Bust & Uneasy Recovery

The dot-com crash in 2001 deflated rents and with them the incentive to push tenants out. Evictions fell for several years — not because of stronger protections, but because the economics had shifted.


By 2007, real estate investment had returned and evictions were climbing again. 2005: 1,878 evictions — the decade's low point.

2013 – 2016

The Tech Surge

The second tech boom was fiercer. Google and Salesforce buses became symbols of a city reshaping itself around highly paid workers. Ellis Act filings — a legal tool to remove tenants by taking buildings off the rental market — hit a 15-year high.


2015: 2,352 evictions. Mission, SoMa, and Western Addition saw the sharpest spikes. Longtime Latino and Black communities bore the heaviest burden.

2020

The Moratorium

COVID-19 arrived in March 2020. California and San Francisco imposed eviction moratoriums. For the first time in the dataset's history, evictions essentially stopped — not because housing had become more affordable, but because the law temporarily prevented it.


2020: 519 evictions — a drop of 83% in a single year. Every neighborhood on the map went pale.

2021 – 2026

Post-COVID: The Return

When protections expired and courts reopened, evictions resumed their upward trajectory. Remote work disrupted some expensive neighborhoods while intensifying pressure on others. The familiar hotspots — Mission, Tenderloin, Western Addition — reappeared.


2023: 1,840 evictions and rising. The question is whether the city has learned anything from the last three decades.
