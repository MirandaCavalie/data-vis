Displaced

27 years of eviction in San Francisco, told one neighborhood at a time

Evictions by Neighborhood

1998
1997 – 2000

The Crisis Begins

San Francisco's first tech boom turned the city upside down. Venture capital flooded in, startups multiplied, and landlords discovered that long-term tenants were an obstacle to higher rents. Eviction notices surged to levels the city had never seen.


1998: 3,865 evictions — a peak that would not be surpassed for over a decade. The Mission, SoMa, and Western Addition were hit hardest.

2001 – 2007

Bust & Uneasy Recovery

When the dot-com bubble burst in 2001, tech workers left the city and rents softened. Eviction counts fell sharply — not because landlords became kinder, but because the financial incentive to push tenants out had temporarily disappeared.


By 2007, though, a new wave of investment was already gathering. The respite was brief. 2005: 1,878 evictions — the lowest in a decade.

2013 – 2016

The Tech Surge

The second tech boom was bigger, faster, and more ruthless. With Google, Facebook, and Uber shuttles crisscrossing the city, neighborhoods close to transit corridors became prime targets. Ellis Act withdrawals — a tool to take rental units off the market entirely — spiked dramatically.


The Mission District and SoMa saw the sharpest increases. 2015: 2,352 evictions citywide, with Ellis Act filings at a 15-year high.

2020

The Moratorium

COVID-19 arrived in March 2020. Within weeks, California imposed an eviction moratorium, and San Francisco added its own protections. For the first time in recorded history, evictions in the city nearly stopped.


2020: 519 evictions — a drop of over 80% from the prior year. The map goes pale. Every neighborhood quieted.

2021 – 2026

Post-COVID: The Return

As moratoriums expired and courts reopened, evictions climbed back. The neighborhoods that had been hotspots before COVID — Mission, Tenderloin, Western Addition — resumed their familiar patterns. Remote work reshaped demand but did not end displacement.


2023: 1,840 evictions — and rising. The question now is whether SF's housing protections are strong enough to prevent another crisis-level surge.

The Story Continues

In 27 years, San Francisco has filed over 48,000 eviction notices. Behind each number is a household, a disrupted life, a neighborhood changed forever.
